# Lens — Framing, Methodology, and Audience Synthesis

**Source:** Working sessions, May 25, 2026
**Status:** Working document. Not yet integrated into bible, Master Document, build guide, or Knight Foundation application.
**Purpose:** Capture the strategic framing, methodology principles, and editorial decisions developed during the Stage 1 cleanup story drafting (CRISPR and dandelion drones), so they can be returned to when revising the Knight application, the Master Document, the project bible, the build guide, and investor materials.

---

## 1. The core reframe

The Lens pitch, as it sits in current materials, leads with bias as the problem and the bias filter as the solution. The three reading levels appear as a supporting feature.

That order is wrong. It puts the smallest claim on top and the largest claims underneath it as supporting evidence.

The correct order inverts the stack. Three nested claims, in this order:

**Claim 1 — Value claim.** Access to substantive information about the world should be universal. It should not be gated by reading level, by education, by time available, or by which adult in a reader's life happened to learn how to read the news.

**Claim 2 — Capability claim.** Lens has built a methodology that delivers on that value. The methodology is the cross-level editorial architecture that maintains substantive accuracy across a sixfold spread in reader sophistication, from age 7 through adult. Nothing else in the news landscape does this.

**Claim 3 — Quality claim.** The methodology works because the inputs are clean. The bias filter is the input quality control that makes the rest of the methodology meaningful. Without it, cross-level accessibility produces ultra-processed content that goes down easier, which is the worst possible outcome.

Claim 2 is the means to Claim 1. Claim 3 is the means to Claim 2. The bias filter is not the headline. It is also not buried. It belongs in the position the methodology requires it to occupy: as the input layer that makes everything downstream trustworthy.

---

## 2. Why this reframe matters

### For the Knight Foundation

Knight's mission centers on informed and engaged communities. A pitch built around "we filter bias" places Lens inside a crowded landscape with AllSides, Ground News, The Factual, Tangle, Improve the News, and others.

A pitch built around "we have built a methodology that makes the same story accessible at three reading levels without losing substance" is a claim no other product in that landscape is making. Ground News does not. Tangle does not. AllSides does not.

The bias filter is necessary infrastructure for the methodology to work. The methodology itself is the differentiated thing.

### For investors and grantmakers more broadly

The bias-filter pitch is a values pitch. Values pitches work on people who already share the values.

The methodology pitch is a capability pitch. Capability pitches work on people who do not yet share the values, because they communicate that the team has built something hard. Hard things are interesting. Values are everywhere.

### For users and parents

The bias-filter framing positions Lens alongside Common Sense Media or YouTube Kids parental controls.

The methodology framing positions Lens as something closer to The Economist for the whole family: the adult and the child reading the same substantive coverage of the same world, calibrated to their respective vocabularies. That is a much more compelling household proposition. It also explains why the product is for everyone, not just for children, in a way the current pitch struggles to.

## 3. The methodology, named

The methodology is the worked-out, repeatable, defensible editorial process that produces cross-level accessibility without losing substance. The principles, surfaced during the CRISPR drafting:

- Define every technical term the first time it appears, then use it consistently.
- Use the real word, not a substitute. "Enzyme" not "tiny worker." "DNA" not "instruction tape."
- Adjust sentence mechanics to hit the Lexile target. Do not adjust concept depth.
- Carry the same perspectives across levels, anchored differently.
- Trust the reader at every level to handle real questions if the writing does the work to support them.
- Use concrete anchors, not analogies that paper over the science.
- Do not soften disturbing content into uselessness. Do not introduce it without context either.
- Preserve the substantive question across levels even when the framing has to change.
- **The first sentence is the lede and the first sentence is the gate.** It has to deliver the news, and it has to be readable by someone with no prior context. Technical terms that require definition cannot appear in the first sentence. If the news cannot be stated in the first sentence without a technical term, the news has to be restated in plain language and the technical term introduced in the second sentence, where it can be defined as it appears. **Young Reader exception:** at Young Reader level, the entire opening is the gate. The reader needs conceptual ground established before any news can land. The structure is explanatory narrative, where the news occurs after the necessary conceptual ground has been laid. This is a different journalistic form, not a relaxation of the lede rule.
- **A Lens story is a system, not a sequence.** A change in any section has implications for every other section. Pressure-test downstream effects before locking any single change. The four sections (whatHappened, whyItMatters, whatPeopleThink, whatWeDontKnow) and the three reading levels together form a single editorial object. A perspective change at the Adult level may require revisions to the same perspective at Teen and Young, to the whatWeDontKnow at all three levels, and to the whatHappened framing if the change shifts emphasis enough.
- **Perspectives at strongest form. No manufactured symmetry.** Include perspectives that actually exist in the reporting. Present each at the strongest version of itself. Do not invent a contrarian voice to create the appearance of balance where the reporting does not support it.
- **Perspectives at strongest form require current-state-of-field accuracy, not just internal coherence.** A perspective that is internally well-argued but built on a stale model of the institutional, technological, or economic landscape is not actually at strongest form. It is the strongest form of a position that has been overtaken by events. Writers and QC reviewers should specifically verify that the landscape described in each perspective reflects current reality. This is particularly important for perspectives that touch funding, regulation, market structure, or institutional roles, which can shift rapidly. (Surfaced when a draft framed Mars funding as exclusively a NASA question, missing the commercial-space-sector role that has become structurally significant in the past decade.)
- **Once a technical term has been defined and used, prefer plain-language back-references in subsequent sections** unless the technical term itself is what is being discussed. This is the inverse of the define-on-first-use rule and serves the same purpose: reduce unnecessary cognitive load on the reader while preserving precision where precision matters. (Example: after "piezoelectric polymers" has been defined in whatHappened, refer to "the drones' power source" in whatWeDontKnow rather than reintroducing the technical term.)
- **Sentence-initial coordinating conjunctions are allowed.** Sentences may begin with "and," "but," or "so" when the construction creates rhythm or signals a logical relationship to the previous sentence. The standard is whether the construction serves the prose, not whether a sixth-grade teacher would have allowed it. Do not overuse, and do not use as a substitute for proper sentence structure.
- **Attribution follows the facts, not editorial convenience.** When a research finding involves multiple institutional contributors, all substantive contributors should be named in the reporting, even when one is more institutionally prestigious or makes for a cleaner narrative. The standard: would a careful reader of the primary source understand the contributions correctly from the Lens story? If the story would lead a reader to attribute the work primarily to one party when the actual contribution was meaningfully shared, the story is wrong. Three failure patterns to watch for: (1) institutional prestige bias (defaulting to the famous university when a less-famous company was a substantive co-contributor); (2) lone-genius framing (collapsing a collaboration to a single named senior author for narrative cleanliness); (3) lede-fitting (preferring a cleaner first sentence over an accurate one). This principle has a Stage 2 implication: the Bias Filter Engine System Prompt must surface all substantive contributors during fact extraction. If fact extraction strips out non-prestigious contributors, the writing step cannot restore them. (Surfaced during the CRISPR story, when the initial draft attributed the work to the Taylor Lab at UT Austin and missed Metagenomi, the biotech company that originally discovered the enzyme and engineered the more efficient variant.)
- **Time language: "recently," "scientists announced," "a new study found"** rather than "today" unless the event literally occurred that day. This applies to all five voices and all three reading levels.

This list belongs in the bible (`/docs/project-bible.md` on the C: drive), in a dedicated Editorial Methodology section. The bible is the document Claude Code reads. Without the methodology in the bible, Stage 2's automated story production will not honor these principles when it begins generating stories at scale.

The three reading levels are not a feature. They are the output of a methodology. That is a different and stronger claim.

## 4. The food framing

Whole foods versus ultra-processed foods is the connective tissue between the bias filter and the accessibility methodology. It explains why they belong together rather than as two separate features.

The framing does three things at once:

- The difference is invisible at the surface. Both are food. Both are news. The difference compounds over time in ways that are hard to undo.
- The difference is not about complexity. Whole foods are not necessarily more complex than processed ones. The difference is about what has been done to the source material between the field and the table.
- The difference matters for everyone, but the effects compound differently at different life stages.

The food framing gives the Knight application a claim about consequence that the current materials gesture at but do not land. Sustained consumption of distorted information has compounding effects on how people understand the world. Lens offers whole-food information at every life stage.

**Deployment rule.** Use the analogy once and use it well. A single sharp deployment in the section that introduces the methodology. Do not sprinkle it through the document. Repeated analogies start to feel like the writer does not have anything else.

## 5. The target audience question

The Lens materials currently have a structural bias toward the youngest user. Cumulative effect: the case for the adult user gets made by implication, as a generalization from the case for the child user, when it should be made on its own terms.

The case for the child user and the case for the adult user are not the same case. Treating them as one weakens both.

### The case for the child user — formation

Children are building their model of how the world works for the first time. Inputs during that period shape the cognitive architecture they will use for the rest of their lives. Distorted inputs during formation produce adults who think in distorted shapes, often without knowing why. The cost is borne over a lifetime and is mostly invisible to the person paying it.

This case is morally weighty and politically resonant. It has an implied passivity: the child is the recipient of whatever the world hands them.

### The case for the adult user — correction, escape, active engagement

Three distinct things, each of which the current Lens framing handles weakly.

**Correction.** Most adults operate on models of how the world works that were assembled from inputs they did not choose, often during their own childhood, often reinforced by decades of consumption of media calibrated to a specific worldview. The model is sticky. Lens presents information in a form that reduces the friction of correction. Stripped of editorial framing, the same facts can land with a reader whose existing model would have rejected them inside the framing of an outlet the reader has decided not to trust. **This is the most important argument for Lens for adults, and the one most absent from the current materials.**

**Escape from echo chambers.** Adult media consumption in 2026 is shaped by recommendation algorithms, social networks, and habit, all of which compound over years into informational worlds that look nothing like each other. The person inside the echo chamber typically does not experience it as a chamber. They experience it as the world as it is. For an adult who suspects they are inside a chamber but cannot easily find the exit, Lens is a tool. For an adult who does not suspect they are inside a chamber, Lens is a way to compare what they thought happened to what survives across sources, and the gap between those two things is the chamber, made visible.

**Active engagement with reality.** Adults vote. They make economic decisions. They hold positions in institutions. They serve on juries. They run for office. They donate to causes. They argue with relatives. They make decisions about their children's schools, their parents' healthcare, their own retirement, their community's zoning. The quality of the information they are working from determines the quality of those decisions, and the consequences extend outward. **This is the public-good argument that Knight Foundation actually exists to fund, and it lives most naturally with adult users.**

### The food framing applied to adults

Adults have been consuming ultra-processed information for decades, often without knowing it. The compounding effect on adults shows up as conviction without basis, anger without target, and inability to talk across the household table about things that used to be discussable.

The framing is strongest when it carries both directions. Ultra-processed information is bad for children during formation and bad for adults during everything else. Lens offers whole-food information at every life stage.

## 6. The founders narrative

The current materials build the founding story around Jackson. Rebecca appears as co-founder and COO, which describes her position but does not establish her as the adult-user case made human.

This is a structural problem, not a cosmetic one. It invites Jackson to be the visionary and Rebecca to be the support staff. That framing is wrong on the facts and it sells the company short.

**The true founding story:** Lens exists because a 9-year-old's instinct about bias met an adult's two-decade understanding of how institutional information environments actually function. Either half alone produces a different and weaker company.

Jackson noticed bias because he had not yet learned to tune it out. Rebecca noticed something different: that the people around her, herself included, had been operating inside increasingly incompatible information environments for years, and that the political and personal costs of that divergence were becoming impossible to ignore.

The product is for both of them. It is for them together, sitting in the same household, reading the same story at different levels, having a real conversation about it.

That is a stronger founder story than "9-year-old has insight, mother helps build it." It is also true.

## 9. Where the rules live, and how they get there

The Lens project has four documents that hold editorial and strategic guidance. Each does a different job. The boundaries have been fuzzy, which is why locked-language decisions ended up only in memory, and why the project bible was missing for all of Stage 1.

### The four documents

1. **`/docs/project-bible.md`** (on the C: drive). The combined product specification and editorial standards document that Claude Code reads. Was missing for Stage 1, reconstructed during May 25 cleanup. This is the operational reference for Claude Code in every build session.

2. **`/docs/bias-filter-prompt.md`** (on the C: drive). The Bias Filter Engine System Prompt. Referenced in Session 25 as "the most important document in the project." Not reviewed in this session. Likely needs methodology principles around perspective construction added.

3. **`Lens_Build_Guide_Sessions_19_to_45_Revised_050326.docx`**. The Stage 2 build guide. Sessions 0-18 complete; this document covers Sessions 19-45. Operational, references the bible for editorial standards.

4. **`Lens_Master_Document_v1_041826_5pm.docx`** (Master Document). The canonical strategic and narrative reference. What humans read to understand what Lens is. The Knight application and other deliverables draw from this document.

### Where each rule belongs

**Editorial rules about story craft** belong in the **bible**. The methodology principles list, the locked language, the lede rule, the system-not-sequence rule, the time-language rule, the Young Reader exception, the perspective construction principles. These govern how every story is written, and Claude Code reads the bible during Stage 2 story production. Without these in the bible, automated story production will not honor them.

**Strategic framing and audience positioning** belong in the **Master Document**. The three-claim nested structure, the food framing, the case for adult users in three strands, the founders narrative correction, the youngest-user-bias diagnosis. These are not rules for how a story is written. They are claims Lens makes about itself in the world. They show up in deliverables (Knight application, investor materials, website copy) that draw from the Master Document.

**Specific session references to editorial methodology** belong in the **build guide for Sessions 19-45**. The bible holds the methodology in full. The build guide points to it at the specific sessions where it has to be operationalized into prompts or checks. Without this, the bible can hold the methodology in pristine form forever and Stage 2 will not honor it.

**Working synthesis** stays in this document. New principles get articulated here, captured immediately, and then promoted to the bible or Master Document during deliberate update passes. This document remains the complete record of the thinking even after principles get promoted.

### Build guide sessions that need methodology references

Reviewed against `Lens_Build_Guide_Sessions_19_to_45_Revised_050326.docx`:

The build guide currently says "Read /docs/project-bible.md for full context" in many session prompts. That instruction is too general to ensure the methodology gets honored. That phrasing signals that the section is load-bearing for the session and not just background.
